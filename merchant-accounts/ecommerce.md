## Understanding eCommerce Payment Processing

Ecommerce payment processing allows merchants to accept payments online when customers make purchases on their websites.

Thanks to advancements in technology, ecommerce merchants now have a plethora of options for accepting payments from customers.

### Setting Up a Merchant Account

The journey begins with creating a merchant account, a special bank account provided by an acquiring bank.

This account temporarily holds customer payments. Once a customer's payment is approved, the funds are transferred from their account to the merchant account.

After deducting transaction and other applicable fees, the remaining funds are transferred to the merchant’s business bank account.

### Merchant Services Providers

A merchant services provider offers both merchant accounts and payment processing services.

While some companies specialize in one area, many business owners prefer a provider that offers comprehensive services to streamline their operations.

### Options for Online Payment Processing

Ecommerce businesses have multiple options for processing payments online, including:

* Payment Gateways: These function like POS systems or credit card terminals, encrypting and forwarding credit card information to the payment processor for approval or denial.
* Virtual Terminals: These allow merchants to accept various payment types using any internet-connected device. Payment information is entered into an online form for processing.
* Shopping Cart Integration: Using the payment processor’s API, merchants can integrate their shopping carts to start accepting payments quickly.
* Mobile Payment Processing: This can involve using a mobile credit card reader or integrating payment processing capabilities into a mobile app.
    

### Merchant Services Provider vs. Payment Aggregator

Payment aggregators like PayPal, Stripe, and Square use a single master merchant account for all their customers. This can pose risks, such as account freezes or closures due to issues like fraud or excessive chargebacks.

In contrast, merchant services providers set up dedicated merchant accounts for each business, offering better protection and stability.

## Key Considerations for Choosing an eCommerce Payment Processor

Before selecting an ecommerce payment processor, consider the following:

* Payment Options: Offering various payment methods (e.g., credit/debit cards, PayPal, digital wallets, etc.) can enhance customer convenience and increase sales.
* Contract Details: Understand the contract length, equipment inclusions, early termination fees, and other fee structures to avoid costly commitments.
* Essential Features: Identify must-have features such as online reporting, integration with accounting software, global currency acceptance, and fraud prevention measures.
    

### Pricing Models

Different pricing models can affect the cost of processing payments:

* Interchange-Plus Pricing: This model combines the interchange rate (set by credit card networks) with an additional fee from the merchant account provider.
* Flat-Rate Pricing: A consistent fee is charged for all transactions, often chosen by new businesses.
* Tiered Rate Pricing: Transactions fall into qualified, mid-qualified, or non-qualified categories, though this model can lack transparency.
    

### Access to Funds and Fraud Prevention

Ensure you understand how quickly funds will be available in your business bank account after processing fees are deducted.

Also, check what fraud prevention measures are in place to protect your business from chargebacks and fraudulent transactions.

### Customer Service and Tech Support

Reliable customer service and technical support are crucial. Ensure the processor you choose offers prompt assistance to resolve any issues that may arise, minimizing the impact on your sales.

By carefully considering these factors, you can select an ecommerce payment processor that best suits your business needs and helps ensure smooth, secure transactions for your customers.
